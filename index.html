Ratana Sutta
रत्तनसुत्त · Khuddaka Nikāya · Theravāda

The Jewel Discourse — one of the most sacred protective chants in Theravāda Buddhism, recited to invoke blessings through the power of truth. It praises the Three Jewels: the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha.

01
The Opening Invocation
Yānīdha bhūtāni samāgatāni — Whatever beings are assembled here
▾
// Context

The sutta opens with an invitation addressed to all beings, both human and non-human, gathered to hear the teaching. The recitation of this sutta is believed to dispel misfortune and invoke protection through the power of truth (sacca-kiriyā).

// Key Verses

Whatever beings are assembled here, on the ground or in the air — may all beings be happy; let them listen carefully to what is said.

— Ratana Sutta, Verse 1 (Snp 2.1)
Read Full Sutta ↗
02
The Jewel of the Buddha
Yaṃ kiñci vittaṃ idha vā huraṃ vā — Whatever wealth there is here or beyond
▾
// Teaching

This section praises the Buddha as the incomparable jewel — the Tathāgata who achieved what no being in the world has achieved, attaining the supreme goal of awakening and compassionately sharing it with all.

Whatever wealth there is, here or beyond, or whatever precious jewel there is in the heavens — there is nothing comparable to the Tathāgata. This too is a precious jewel in the Buddha. By this truth, may there be well-being.

— Ratana Sutta, Verse 2
Read Full Sutta ↗
03
The Jewel of the Dhamma
Khayaṃ virāgaṃ amataṃ paṇītaṃ — The destruction, dispassion, the deathless, the excellent
▾
// Teaching

The Dhamma — the teaching of the Buddha — is described as that which leads to the destruction of craving, dispassion, the deathless, and the excellent state of Nibbāna. It is the second precious jewel.

The destruction of craving, dispassion, the deathless, the excellent — what the Sakyan Sage attained in concentration — nothing is equal to this Dhamma. This too is a precious jewel in the Dhamma. By this truth, may there be well-being.

— Ratana Sutta, Verse 5
Read Full Sutta ↗
04
The Jewel of the Saṅgha
Ye puggalā aṭṭha sataṃ pasatthā — The eight persons praised by the good
▾
// Teaching

The Noble Saṅgha — the four pairs of noble persons who have entered the stream, are once-returners, non-returners, and Arahants — is praised as the supreme field of merit, the third jewel worthy of offerings and veneration.

The eight persons praised by the good — the four pairs — they are the disciples of the Blessed One, worthy of gifts, hospitality, offerings, and reverential salutation. They are an incomparable field of merit for the world.

— Ratana Sutta, Verse 7

Metta Sutta
Karaṇīyamettā Sutta · Sutta Nipāta 1.8 · Theravāda

The Discourse on Loving-Kindness — the Buddha's sublime instruction on how to cultivate boundless goodwill (mettā) toward all living beings without exception. One of the most beloved and most recited texts in Theravāda Buddhism.

01
What Should Be Done
Karaṇīyam atthakusalena — What should be done by one skilled in good
▾
// Context

The sutta opens by describing the qualities of one who is skilled in what is good — upright, gentle, humble, content, easy to support, without conceit. These are the ethical foundations from which loving-kindness naturally arises.

What should be done by one skilled in good, who wants to attain the state of peace: they should be able, upright, very upright, compliant, gentle, not proud.

— Metta Sutta, Verse 1 (Snp 1.8)
02
Radiating Loving-Kindness
Mettañca sabbalokasmiṃ — And loving-kindness to all the world
▾
// The Practice

The heart of the sutta — the instruction to radiate boundless loving-kindness in all directions, like a mother protecting her only child with her own life. This is the brahma-vihāra meditation, the divine abiding of loving-kindness.

Just as a mother would protect her only child with her own life, even so let one cultivate a boundless love towards all beings. Let thoughts of boundless love pervade the whole world — above, below, and across — without any obstruction, without any hatred, without any enmity.

— Metta Sutta, Verses 7–8
03
The Result — No More Rebirth
Na ca khuddaṃ samācare kiñci — And not doing any slight wrong
▾
// The Fruit

The sutta concludes with the promise that one who dwells in the brahma-vihāra of loving-kindness, abandoning wrong views and practicing virtue, will not be reborn in any lower state.

Not falling into wrong views, but being virtuous and endowed with insight, having removed attachment to sensual pleasures — such a person will certainly never again undergo rebirth.

— Metta Sutta, Verse 10

Maitri Sutra
मैत्री सूत्र · Mahāyāna Tradition · Sanskrit

The Mahāyāna counterpart to the Pāli Metta Sutta — the Maitri Sutra expands the practice of loving-kindness (maitrī) into the Bodhisattva ideal, cultivating boundless compassion for all sentient beings across all realms of existence.

01
Maitrī — The First Brahma-Vihāra
मैत्री — Loving-kindness as universal friendliness
▾
// Teaching

In the Mahāyāna understanding, maitrī is the sincere wish for all beings to have happiness and the causes of happiness. It is the first of the four immeasurables (apramāṇa) and the foundation of Bodhicitta — the awakening mind.

May all beings have happiness and the causes of happiness. May all beings be free from suffering and the causes of suffering. May all beings never be separated from joy that is free of suffering. May all beings dwell in equanimity, free from attachment and aversion.

— The Four Immeasurables Prayer, Tibetan tradition
02
The Bodhisattva's Vow
बोधिचित्त — The awakening mind dedicated to all
▾
// The Mahāyāna Expansion

In Mahāyāna, loving-kindness is inseparable from the Bodhisattva vow — the commitment to remain in saṃsāra until all sentient beings have been liberated. This transforms individual practice into a cosmic act of compassion.

However innumerable sentient beings are, I vow to save them all. However inexhaustible the afflictions are, I vow to extinguish them all. However immeasurable the teachings are, I vow to master them all. However incomparable Buddhahood is, I vow to attain it.

— The Four Bodhisattva Vows, Zen tradition

Prajñāpāramitā Sūtras
प्रज्ञापारमिता · Perfection of Wisdom · Mahāyāna

The Perfection of Wisdom texts — including the Heart Sūtra and Diamond Sūtra — are the foundational Mahāyāna scriptures on śūnyatā (emptiness). They reveal that all phenomena are empty of inherent self-existence, liberating the practitioner from all conceptual grasping.

01
Heart Sūtra — Form is Emptiness
Prajñāpāramitāhṛdayasūtra · 260 characters
▾
// The Central Teaching

The Heart Sūtra is the most condensed and most recited text in Mahāyāna Buddhism. In just 260 Chinese characters, it encapsulates the entire Prajñāpāramitā teaching. Avalokiteśvara, the Bodhisattva of Compassion, reveals to Śāriputra the nature of emptiness.

Form is emptiness, emptiness is form. Emptiness is not other than form; form is not other than emptiness. In the same way, feeling, perception, mental formation, and consciousness are emptiness.

— Heart Sūtra (Prajñāpāramitāhṛdayasūtra)
02
Diamond Sūtra — No Fixed Dharma
Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā · The Thunderbolt Cutter
▾
// The Teaching

The Diamond Sūtra is a dialogue between the Buddha and his disciple Subhūti on the nature of non-attachment and the perfection of wisdom. The Buddha repeatedly deconstructs Subhūti's conceptual views through a series of paradoxical statements.

All conditioned phenomena are like a dream, an illusion, a bubble, a shadow, like dew or a flash of lightning. Thus should they be contemplated.

— Diamond Sūtra, Final Verse (Vajracchedikā)

Dhammapada
ධම්මපද · Verses of the Dhamma · Khuddaka Nikāya

423 verses of distilled Buddhist wisdom organized in 26 chapters — the most beloved text in the entire Pāli Canon. Each verse is a gem of ethical and philosophical insight, beginning with the famous declaration that mind is the forerunner of all actions.

01
Yamakavagga — The Pairs
Chapter 1 · Verses 1–20
▾
// Opening Verses

The Dhammapada opens with the most famous Buddhist teaching on the primacy of mind. The two opening verses form a perfect pair — showing how both suffering and happiness arise from the quality of one's mental states and intentions.

Mind is the forerunner of all actions. All deeds are led by mind, created by mind. If one speaks or acts with a corrupt mind, suffering follows, as the wheel follows the hoof of an ox.

— Dhammapada, Verse 1

Mind is the forerunner of all actions. All deeds are led by mind, created by mind. If one speaks or acts with a serene mind, happiness follows, as a shadow that never departs.

— Dhammapada, Verse 2
02
Appamādavagga — Heedfulness
Chapter 2 · Verses 21–32
▾
// Key Verses

Heedfulness (appamāda) — the opposite of negligence and carelessness — is declared to be the path to the deathless. It is the quality of diligent, careful attention that the Buddha considered the foundation of all spiritual progress.

Heedfulness is the path to the deathless. Heedlessness is the path to death. The heedful do not die. The heedless are as if already dead.

— Dhammapada, Verse 21
03
Cittavagga — The Mind
Chapter 3 · Verses 33–43
▾
// On Taming the Mind

Chapter 3 focuses on the nature of the mind — its tendency to wander, and the extraordinary benefit of training and taming it. The tamed mind brings happiness; the untamed mind brings suffering.

The mind is wavering and restless, difficult to guard and difficult to control. The wise person straightens it as a fletcher straightens an arrow.

— Dhammapada, Verse 33

Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta
Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta · Dīgha Nikāya 22 · Theravāda

The Discourse on the Four Foundations of Mindfulness — declared by the Buddha to be the direct path for the purification of beings, the overcoming of sorrow and lamentation, and the realization of Nibbāna.

01
Kāyānupassanā — Mindfulness of Body
The first foundation of mindfulness
▾
// The Practice

The first foundation is mindfulness of the body — beginning with mindfulness of breathing (ānāpānasati), then extending to the four postures, clear comprehension in all activities, contemplation of the body's 32 parts, the four elements, and charnel ground contemplations.

Here, a practitioner, having gone to the forest or to the root of a tree or to an empty place, sits down cross-legged, holds their body erect, and sets up mindfulness in front of them. Mindful they breathe in, mindful they breathe out.

— Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta, Ānāpāna section
02
Vedanānupassanā — Mindfulness of Feelings
The second foundation of mindfulness
▾
// Feeling Tone

The second foundation is mindfulness of vedanā — the feeling tone of pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral that accompanies every experience. Insight into vedanā reveals how craving arises and how it can be stopped.

When feeling a pleasant feeling, they know: "I feel a pleasant feeling." When feeling an unpleasant feeling, they know: "I feel an unpleasant feeling." When feeling a neutral feeling, they know: "I feel a neutral feeling."

— Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta, Vedanā section
03
Dhammānupassanā — Mindfulness of Phenomena
The fourth foundation · The Five Hindrances & Seven Factors
▾
// The Highest Foundation

The fourth foundation encompasses mindfulness of mental objects — the Five Hindrances, Five Aggregates, Six Sense Bases, Seven Factors of Awakening, and the Four Noble Truths. It culminates in direct insight into the nature of reality.

And what are the seven factors of awakening? Here, a practitioner develops the awakening factor of mindfulness … investigation of phenomena … energy … rapture … tranquility … concentration … equanimity — based on seclusion, dispassion, and cessation, ripening in release.

— Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta, Bojjhaṅga section

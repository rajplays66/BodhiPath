The Dhamma

Core Teachings

I
Cattāri Ariyasaccāni
The Four Noble Truths
Cattāri Ariyasaccāni — Pāli Canon
▾

The Four Noble Truths form the Buddha's first and most fundamental teaching, delivered at Deer Park in Varanasi. They constitute a complete diagnosis of the human condition and a prescription for liberation — modelled on the ancient Indian medical framework of identifying a disease, its cause, prognosis, and cure.

  • Dukkha — The truth of suffering: life is permeated by unsatisfactoriness, from gross pain to subtle dissatisfaction
  • Samudāya — The origin of suffering: craving (taṇhā) arising from ignorance is the root cause
  • Nirodha — The cessation of suffering: complete liberation is possible — this is Nibbāna
  • Magga — The path: the Noble Eightfold Path leads to the cessation of suffering

II
Ariyo Aṭṭhaṅgiko Maggo
The Noble Eightfold Path
Ariyo Aṭṭhaṅgiko Maggo
▾

The Noble Eightfold Path is the practical prescription for ending suffering — the fourth of the Four Noble Truths. Grouped into three divisions of wisdom (paññā), ethical conduct (sīla), and mental cultivation (samādhi), it provides a complete guide to liberation.

  • Sammā Diṭṭhi — Right Understanding: seeing reality as it is, particularly the Four Noble Truths
  • Sammā Saṅkappa — Right Intention: renunciation, non-ill-will, non-cruelty
  • Sammā Vācā — Right Speech: truthful, harmonious, gentle, meaningful
  • Sammā Kammanta — Right Action: abstaining from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct
  • Sammā Ājīva — Right Livelihood: earning a living in a harmless, honest way
  • Sammā Vāyāma — Right Effort: cultivating wholesome states, abandoning unwholesome ones
  • Sammā Sati — Right Mindfulness: clear awareness of body, feelings, mind, and phenomena
  • Sammā Samādhi — Right Concentration: the four jhānas of meditative absorption

III
Tilakkhaṇa
The Three Marks of Existence
Anicca · Dukkha · Anattā
▾

The Three Marks of Existence (tilakkhaṇa) are three characteristics that the Buddha declared to be present in all conditioned phenomena. Insight into these three characteristics is central to the path of liberation in all Buddhist traditions.

  • Anicca (Impermanence) — All conditioned things are in a constant state of flux and change; nothing lasts
  • Dukkha (Unsatisfactoriness) — Because of impermanence, clinging to what is impermanent leads to suffering
  • Anattā (Non-self) — There is no permanent, unchanging self or soul in any conditioned phenomenon

IV
Paṭicca-samuppāda
Dependent Origination
Imasmiṃ sati idaṃ hoti — This being, that arises
▾

Paṭicca-samuppāda — Dependent Origination — is one of the most profound and central teachings in all of Buddhism. It describes the twelve-linked chain of causation that perpetuates the cycle of rebirth (saṃsāra) and explains how suffering arises and ceases.

  • With ignorance (avijjā) as condition, volitional formations (saṅkhāra) arise
  • With formations as condition, consciousness (viññāṇa) arises
  • The chain continues through name-and-form, the six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, clinging, becoming, birth
  • With birth as condition: aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief and despair arise

V
Kamma
Karma & Intentional Action
Cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmi — Intention, monks, is what I call karma
▾

In Buddhism, kamma (Sanskrit: karma) refers specifically to intentional action — mental, verbal, or physical — and its potential to bear fruit (kammavipāka) in the future. The Buddha radically redefined the Brahmanical concept by placing intention at the center.

  • Wholesome kamma (kusala): actions rooted in non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion
  • Unwholesome kamma (akusala): actions rooted in greed (lobha), hatred (dosa), or delusion (moha)
  • Null kamma: actions that generate no kammic fruit, e.g. actions of an Arahant

VI
Nibbāna / Nirvāṇa
Liberation & the Unconditioned
Atthi bhikkhave ajātaṃ abhūtaṃ akataṃ asaṅkhataṃ
▾

Nibbāna (Sanskrit: Nirvāṇa) is the supreme goal of Buddhist practice — the complete extinguishing of craving, aversion, and delusion, and the cessation of the entire mass of suffering. The Buddha described it as the unconditioned, the unborn, the unbecome, the unmade.

  • Nibbāna with remainder (sa-upādisesa-nibbāna): liberation attained while still alive — the Arahant
  • Nibbāna without remainder (anupādisesa-nibbāna): liberation at the death of the Arahant — Parinibbāna

Dharma Glossary

Ānāpānasati
Pāli · Mindfulness of breathing

Meditation on the breath as the primary object of mindfulness — the most direct path to calming the mind and developing jhāna.
Bodhicitta
Sanskrit · Awakening mind

The altruistic intention to attain Buddhahood for the benefit of all sentient beings — the defining aspiration of the Bodhisattva.
Dukkha
Pāli · Suffering / unsatisfactoriness

The first of the Three Marks of Existence — encompasses suffering, dissatisfaction, imperfection, and the unsatisfactory nature of conditioned phenomena.
Śūnyatā
Sanskrit · Emptiness

The central Mahāyāna teaching that all phenomena are empty of inherent self-existence — they arise dependently, without independent, fixed essence.
Upāya
Sanskrit · Skillful means

The ability to adapt the Buddha's teaching to the needs and capacities of different beings — a key concept in Mahāyāna doctrine.
Tathāgatagarbha
Sanskrit · Buddha-nature

The innate Buddha-nature present in all sentient beings — the seed of awakening that awaits recognition and cultivation.
Prajñā
Sanskrit · Wisdom

Insight wisdom — direct understanding of the three characteristics of existence, culminating in the penetration of Nirvāṇa.
Saṅgha
Pāli/Sanskrit · Community

The community of Buddhist practitioners — one of the Three Jewels. In the highest sense: the assembly of Noble Ones who have attained liberation.

2600 Years

History of Buddhism

c. 563–483 BCE
Birth & Enlightenment of the Buddha

Siddhārtha Gautama attains supreme enlightenment under the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gayā and begins teaching the Dhamma.

c. 483 BCE
First Buddhist Council

500 Arahants convene at Rājagaha after the Buddha's Parinibbāna to recite and preserve the Dhamma and Vinaya.

c. 268–232 BCE
Emperor Aśoka's Patronage

The Mauryan Emperor Aśoka converts to Buddhism and sends missionaries across Asia, inscribing Dharma edicts on pillars across his empire.

c. 1st Century CE
Rise of Mahāyāna Buddhism

New sūtras appear, expanding the scope of liberation to all beings — the Bodhisattva ideal becomes central to a new movement.

7th–8th Century CE
Vajrayāna in Tibet

Padmasambhava introduces tantric Buddhism to Tibet, founding the Nyingma school. The Tibetan Canon begins to be compiled.

19th–20th Century
Buddhism Comes to the West

Through colonial scholarship, emigration, and figures like D.T. Suzuki and Thich Nhat Hanh, Buddhism takes root across Europe and the Americas.
